Report Writing Guide

A practical, interactive tool to help you structure, write, and refine professional reports using Plain English.

Before You Start

Don't start writing yet. A good report solves a specific problem for a specific person. Answer these three questions to clarify your thinking.

Why are you writing this? What is the outcome?

Who will read this? What do they already know?

What are the boundaries? What will you NOT cover?

Standard Report Structure

Click on the report sections below to understand what belongs in each part.

Crucial

Executive Summary

This is the most important part of your report. Many busy executives will only read this section.

What to include:

  • The purpose of the report.
  • The key findings or problems identified.
  • The main recommendations.

Tip: Write this section last, after you have finished the rest of the report. Keep it under one page.

Introduction

Sets the scene for the reader. It explains why the report exists and what it covers.

Key elements:

  • Background: Briefly explain the context or history.
  • Purpose: Why was this report written?
  • Scope: What areas are covered (and what are not).
  • Methodology: How did you get your information? (e.g., surveys, interviews, desktop research).

Body (Findings & Discussion)

This is where you present your evidence. Organise this section logically, using clear headings.

How to structure it:

  • Use numbered headings (e.g., 2.1, 2.2).
  • Group related information together.
  • Present facts objectively.
  • Use charts, tables, and lists to break up dense text.

Remember: Analyse the facts—don't just list them. Explain what the data means.

Conclusion

Summarises the main points derived from the body. There should be no new information here.

Checklist:

  • Does it follow logically from your findings?
  • Does it answer the main question posed in the introduction?
  • Is it concise and clear?
Actionable

Recommendations

Specific actions the reader should take based on your conclusion.

Make them SMART:

  • Specific: Start with a verb (e.g., "Purchase", "Hire", "Update").
  • Measurable: How will we know it's done?
  • Achievable: Can we actually do this?
  • Realistic: Do we have the resources?
  • Time-bound: When should it happen?

Example: "Update the safety manual by 30 June."

Writing in Plain English

Plain English is faster to write and easier to read. It is not "dumbing down"—it is about communicating complex ideas clearly.

Avoid (Bureaucratic)

"It is requested that all staff members proceed to the undertaking of the completion of the attached forms by the close of business."

  • Passive voice ("It is requested")
  • Wordy ("proceed to the undertaking")
  • Formal jargon ("close of business")

Use (Plain English)

"Please fill in the attached forms by 5 pm today."

  • Active voice ("Please fill in")
  • Direct verbs
  • Specific time ("5 pm")
